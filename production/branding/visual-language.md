# Visual Language

Articulate media should feel like an architectural journal made visible: precise, calm, thoughtful and production-oriented.

The canonical rules for applying this identity across scenes, shots and episodes are defined in the [Articulate Visual Grammar](../../docs/media/visual-grammar.md). This document establishes the broad visual direction; the grammar governs editorial composition, progression and rhythm.

## Direction

- Use restrained layouts with clear hierarchy.
- Prefer diagrams, repository views, journal pages and sketches over decorative imagery.
- Use text fragments sparingly and only when they sharpen the argument.
- Keep slides structural rather than promotional.
- Leave room for uncertainty, questions and trade-offs.

## Colour and Contrast

The visual system should support readability first. Colours should distinguish concepts, states and relationships rather than create spectacle. Contrast must be strong enough for video compression and small-screen viewing.

## Typography

On-screen text should be short, legible and purposeful. Avoid dense paragraphs. Use titles, labels, questions, excerpts and diagrams to support the narration.

## Diagrams

Diagrams should make architectural relationships easier to understand. They should favour explicit labels, simple shapes, visible direction and clear boundaries. When a diagram represents an interpretation rather than a source fact, that should be recorded in the storyboard or asset register.

## Repository and Journal Views

Screen captures of the repository, website or journal should be used as evidence and orientation. Avoid fast scrolling or ornamental terminal footage. Show the thing that supports the point.

## Companion Integration

The companion should not dominate the frame. It should appear when a presenter presence helps guide the viewer, introduce a section, connect ideas or close an episode.

## Avoid

- generic corporate stock visuals;
- excessive animation;
- dense slide decks;
- decorative complexity;
- misleading photorealism;
- visual metaphors that add claims not present in the episode.
